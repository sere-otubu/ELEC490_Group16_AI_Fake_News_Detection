SEARCH_TOPIC: Salmonella
TITLE: <i>Salmonella-</i>induced SIRT1 and SIRT3 are crucial for maintaining the metabolic switch in bacteria and host for successful pathogenesis.
PMID: 39693143
SOURCE_URL: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/39693143/
TYPE: PubMed Medical Abstract

ABSTRACT:
Sirtuins are the major players in host immunometabolic regulation. However, the role of sirtuins in the modulation of the immune metabolism pertaining to salmonellosis is largely unknown. Here, our investigation focussed on the role of two important sirtuins, SIRT1 and SIRT3, shedding light on their impact on intracellular <i>Salmonella</i>'s metabolic switch and pathogenesis establishment. Our study indicated the ability of the live <i>Salmonella</i> Typhimurium to differentially regulate the levels of SIRT1 and SIRT3 for maintaining the high glycolytic metabolism and low fatty acid metabolism in <i>Salmonella</i>. Perturbing SIRT1 or SIRT3 through knockdown or inhibition resulted in a remarkable shift in the host metabolism to low fatty acid oxidation and high glycolysis. This switch led to decreased proliferation of <i>Salmonella</i> in the macrophages. Further, <i>Salmonella</i>-induced higher levels of SIRT1 and SIRT3 led to a skewed polarization state of the macrophages from a pro-inflammatory M1 state toward an immunosuppressive M2, making it more conducive for the intracellular life of <i>Salmonella</i>. Alongside, governing immunological functions by modulating p65 NF-κB acetylation, SIRT1, and SIRT3 also skew <i>Salmonella-</i>induced host metabolic switch by regulating the acetylation status of HIF-1α and PDHA1. Interestingly, though knockdown of SIRT1/3 attenuated <i>Salmonella</i> proliferation in macrophages, in in vivo mice model of infection, inhibition or knockdown of SIRT1/3 led to more dissemination and higher organ burden, which can be attributed to enhanced ROS and IL-6 production. Our study hence reports for the first time that <i>Salmonella</i> modulates SIRT1/3 levels to maintain its own metabolism for successful pathogenesis.
